1. Project Assignment

1.1 Context
In the Afro-hair care community, "protective styling" (braids, locs, twists) is a fundamental practice. However, a persistent problem remains: the inability to accurately estimate how many packs of hair extensions are required. Users currently rely on inconsistent social media advice, leading to over-purchasing (wasted money) or under-purchasing (running out of hair mid-process). This project aims to digitize this experience-based knowledge through a 3D web application.

1.2 Project Goal
The goal is to move beyond the Semester 2 Proof of Concept (PoC) into a professional, interactive tool that allows users to:
Visualize hairstyles on a 3D head model in real-time.
Receive an accurate calculation of required hair packs based on specific density and style parameters.
Reduce the stress and financial waste associated with manual estimation.

1.5 User Personas
I have developed three distinct user personas, based on user interviews conducted during semester 1 at Fontys, that capture the technical, cultural, and professional diversity of the target audience. This includes the specific addition of a male user to address short-style visualization and terminology barriers.

Persona 1: DIY Braider "Budget Strategist"
Profile: Maya, a 22-year-old university student.
Context: Maya braids her own hair to save money. She often finds herself at the beauty supply store guessing how many packs of "X-pression" hair she needs.
Pain Point: She frequently runs out of hair halfway through her head (back-of-the-head panic) or buys two packs too many that sit in her closet for months.
Tech Needs: Mobile-first interface; needs to see how "Small" vs. "Medium" knotless braids affect the pack count.
Persona 2: "Visual Explorer"
Profile: Jordan, a 25-year-old male exploring natural hair growth.
Context: Jordan is transitioning from a fade to short twists or "starter locs." He doesn't know the difference between "two-strand twists" and "finger coils", he just knows what he wants it to look like.
Pain Point: Difficulty communicating with stylists due to lack of terminology. He wants to know if his current hair density is enough for a specific look.
Tech Needs: Visual presets (choosing by image/3D model rather than text); focus on shorter hair lengths and scalp visibility (parting patterns).
Persona 3: Professional Stylist
Profile: Sarah, owner of a specialized Afro-hair boutique in Eindhoven.
Context: Sarah sees 4-6 clients a day. She uses the tool during consultations to provide accurate price quotes based on material usage.
Pain Point: Clients often bring the wrong type or insufficient amount of hair to appointments, causing delays and friction.
Tech Needs: High accuracy; ability to show clients the "fullness" of a style virtually before starting the 6-hour labor process.

2. Approach and Planning
2.1 Approach
The project follows an Agile methodology with a significant pivot occurring in Week 10.
Pivot Justification: Initial development was delayed due to "planning paralysis" caused by an overly complex architectural end-goal. To maintain momentum and meet Semester 3 requirements, the project was refocused on a "Utility-First" PoC.
Strategy Shift: The focus shifted from achieving perfection to iterative functional delivery, learning to build and validate core 3D systems even if they require further optimization later. This allowed for a condensed, high-intensity development cycle starting in the second half of the semester.
Research Methods
Prototyping: Testing different Raycasting intervals to balance visual density with performance.
Expert Interview: Structured interviews with Eindhoven-based Afro-hairstylists to validate the calculation formula.
Literature/Tutorial Review: Deep dive into R3F and InstancedMesh documentation (building on previous semester Three.js study).
Project Breakdown
Phase 1: Asset preparation, GLTF optimization, and GitHub environment setup.
Phase 2: Developing the "Parting" algorithm and UV-based spawn logic.
Phase 3: Glassmorphism interface implementation with Tailwind CSS v4.

3. Technical Architecture Diagram 

3.1 System Architecture
The system architecture defines the relationship between the client-side UI, the state management layer, and the 3D rendering engine.

Core Tech Stack
Frontend Framework: React 19 (UI Logic & Component Lifecycle)
State Management: Zustand (Global store for hairstyle parameters)
3D Engine: React-Three-Fiber & Three.js (Procedural mesh generation)
Build Tool: Node.js (Fast HMR and optimized bundling)
Deployment: Vercel (CI/CD and public hosting)

Planned Architectural Layers
Presentation Layer: React components handling the Glassmorphism UI and slider inputs.
State Layer: A centralized Zustand store that acts as the "Single Source of Truth" for hair density, length, and style coefficients.
Logic Layer: The Calculation Engine that processes the mathematical formula and the Raycasting algorithm for mesh placement.
Graphics Layer: The R3F Canvas where the optimized GLB bust and instanced hair segments are rendered.
Purpose 
The architecture is designed to be decoupled and performant:
Decoupling: By separating the Calculation Engine from the Rendering Layer, the mathematical estimates can update instantly even if the 3D scene is still processing complex mesh updates.
Performance: Leveraging a "push-based" state update (Zustand) ensures that only the necessary 3D components re-render when a slider is moved, maintaining the target 30+ FPS.
Scalability: The structure allows for an easy transition from localStorage to a backend database (Supabase) in future iterations without refactoring the core rendering logic.

3.5 Rendering Strategy Comparison
Two procedural rendering approaches are currently being explored during development.
Comparison Table


Feature
Option 1: Raycasting + UV Masking (Testing Now)
Option 2: Predefined Spawn Points (Considered alternative)
Description
Raycasting projects points onto the scalp; UV maps define "White" (spawn) and "Black" (parting) zones.
Static coordinates are manually or procedurally pre-placed on the scalp mesh.
Advantages
High Dynamism: Procedural placement; realistic parting; highly scalable for new styles.
High Stability: Simple implementation; predictable performance; easy debugging.
Challenges
Higher GPU complexity; harder to debug; potential bottleneck on mobile.
Less realistic; "stiff" parting patterns; requires significant manual setup for each head model.
Mobile Strategy
Lower raycast intervals; dynamic density reduction; InstancedMesh optimization.
Adjust visible instances dynamically based on slider state.
Slider Sync
Sliders live-calculate the Raycast grid density and UV sampling frequency.
Sliders simply toggle the visibility of existing coordinate points.


Development Decision
Both methods will be prototyped during development to determine the best balance between:
Visual quality
Real-time performance
Development scope
Semester time constraints

4.8 Comparative Analysis Summary
Comparative research was conducted during the previous semester to evaluate existing hairstyle estimation and inspiration tools.

Existing Solution
User Goal
Common User Frustration
Limitation Compared to This Project
Online Hair Calculators
Quickly estimate how many hair packs are needed before purchasing extensions.
Estimates feel generic and inconsistent between websites. Users cannot visualize the final hairstyle result.
No visualization of style or parting, low personalization, limited control. Results may vary depending on hair packs bought.
Social Media / Forum Feedback (Reddit, TikTok, Discord)
Receive advice from experienced users or stylists who have done similar hairstyles.
Answers are subjective, contradictory, and dependent on personal experience. Users may receive multiple conflicting recommendations.
No standardized calculation system or interactive visualization process. Intrinsic knowledge and terminology between recurring user. Not beginner friendly.
YouTube Tutorials
Follow hairstyle tutorials and estimate materials based on creator demonstrations.
Difficult to translate tutorial results to the user's own head size, density, or preferred braid thickness.
Tutorials are passive learning tools and result may vary depending on the user’s own hair type and skills.
Pinterest Inspiration Boards
Collect hairstyle references and communicate aesthetic preferences to stylists.
Users can save inspiration images but still do not know how many packs are required or whether the style is achievable within budget.
Focused on inspiration only, without estimation accuracy.
Hairstyle Catalog / Curation Websites
Browse categorized hairstyle collections and compare style variations.
Categories are often arbitrary or trend-based, making it difficult to estimate practical requirements such as time, density, or material usage.
One image per style, no pack estimation or hair color recommendation.

Comparative Conclusion
At the time of research, no existing platform combined:
Real-time 3D visualization
Procedural hairstyle generation
Interactive density controls
Hair pack estimation
Afro-textured hairstyle specialization
This creates a unique opportunity for innovation within the project scope.
